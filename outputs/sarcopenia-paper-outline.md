## Abstract (250-300 words)

**Background:** Sarcopenia, the age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass and function, lacks disease-modifying pharmacological treatments. Mitophagy—the selective autophagy of dysfunctional mitochondria—has emerged as a promising therapeutic target.

**Objective:** To evaluate the current evidence for mitophagy-activating compounds as sarcopenia interventions, with emphasis on human clinical data.

**Methods:** Narrative review of preclinical and clinical studies on mitophagy activators in skeletal muscle aging.

**Results:** Urolithin A has the most advanced clinical evidence (2 RCTs, n≈120) showing mitochondrial biomarker improvements, but no functional sarcopenia outcomes. Spermidine has strong epidemiological support but no dedicated muscle trials. Novel compounds remain preclinical. Critical translational gaps exist across all candidates.

**Conclusions:** Mitophagy activation is mechanistically plausible for sarcopenia treatment, but clinical evidence remains insufficient to support therapeutic recommendations outside research settings.

## 1. Introduction (500-600 words)

### 1.1 Sarcopenia: Definition and Clinical Significance
- EWGSOP2 definition: "low muscle strength + low muscle quality/quantity"
- Prevalence: ~10% in adults >60 years, rising to >50% in nonagenarians
- Consequences: falls, frailty, hospitalization, mortality
- Current treatments: resistance exercise (gold standard), protein supplementation (limited efficacy)

### 1.2 Mitochondrial Dysfunction in Muscle Aging
- Accumulation of damaged mitochondria with age
- ROS emission increase, ATP production decline
- Mitochondrial DNA deletion accumulation
- Impaired proteostasis

### 1.3 Mitophagy as a Therapeutic Target
- Definition: selective autophagic removal of damaged mitochondria
- PINK1/Parkin pathway, BNIP3/NIX pathway, FUNDC1 pathway
- The "mitochondrial quality control" concept
- Rationale: restoring mitophagy → improved muscle function

### 1.4 Objective and Scope
- Focus on pharmacological mitophagy activation
- Exclude: exercise, nutritional interventions (protein)
- Clinical translation focus

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Urolithin A (400-500 words)

#### 3.1.1 Mechanism of Action
- Gut microbiome-derived metabolite from ellagitannins
- PINK1/Parkin pathway activation
- Induction of mitophagic flux in C2C12 myotubes
- Dose: 500-1000 mg/day in trials

#### 3.1.2 Human Clinical Evidence
| Study | Design | n | Duration | Key Findings |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Singh et al. (2019) | RCT, crossover | 66 | 4 wk | ↑ mitochondrial gene expression, ↑ plasma acylcarnitines |
| Dethloff et al. (2024) | RCT | 48 | 8 wk | ↑ muscle mitochondrial respiration (PBMCs), improved bioenergetics |
| **Limitations** | No sarcopenia criteria, PBMC only, surrogate endpoints |

#### 3.1.3 Safety Profile
- GRAS status (FDA)
- No serious adverse events in trials up to 8 weeks
- Inter-individual variability (gut microbiome dependent)

#### 3.1.4 Critical Assessment
- Strengths: most advanced clinical development, plausible mechanism
- Weaknesses: no functional endpoints, PBMC biomarkers ≠ muscle mitophagy

### 3.2 Spermidine (300-400 words)

#### 3.2.1 Mechanism of Action
- Polyamine compound
- eIF5A hypusination → TFEB activation → autophagy gene expression
- EP300 inhibition → increased autophagy
- Sources: wheat germ, fermented foods, dietary supplementation

#### 3.2.2 Evidence Base
| Evidence Type | Key Findings |
|---|---|
| Lifespan studies | Extension in yeast, flies, worms, mice |
| Epidemiological | Inverse mortality association (Mediterranean diet studies) |
| Human RCTs | Cognitive benefits, cardiovascular risk reduction |
| Muscle-specific | **NONE** — no dedicated sarcopenia trial exists |

#### 3.2.3 Critical Assessment
- Mechanistically plausible but non-selective (global autophagy)
- Zero dedicated muscle trials = fundamental evidence gap
- Requires Phase II/III trial in sarcopenic patients

### 3.3 Novel Mitophagy Activators (300-400 words)

#### 3.3.1 NAD+ Boosters
- **Nicotinamide Riboside (NR) / NMN**
- → ↑ NAD+ → SIRT1/3 activation → FOXO3/PGC-1α → mitophagy
- Human trial: improved muscle mitochondrial function (n=12, pilot)
- Limitation: small n, no sarcopenia criteria

#### 3.3.2 AMPK Activators
- **Berberine**: AMPK activation → ULK1 → mitophagy
- **Honokiol**: Magnolia bark extract, AMPK-ROS pathway
- Limited human muscle data

#### 3.3.3 Emerging Candidates
- **Fisetin**: Flavonoid, senolytic + mitophagy effects
- **EGCG**: Green tea polyphenol, AMPK/Nrf2 pathways
- **Thymol/Carvacrol**: Essential oil components (Nature Aging 2025)
  - Zebrafish screen → preclinical mitophagy activation
  - No human muscle data yet

#### 3.3.4 Direct Mitophagy Modulators
- PINK1 stabilizers (under development)
- Parkin activators
- BNIP3/NIX pathway modulators
- All preclinical stage

## 4. Discussion (600-800 words)

### 4.1 Synthesis of Evidence
- Table 1: Comparative evidence across compounds
- Best-supported: Urolithin A (clinical), Spermidine (epidemiological)
- Gap: no compound has demonstrated functional sarcopenia improvement

### 4.2 Translational Challenges

#### 4.2.1 Biomarker Validity
- PBMC mitochondrial markers ≠ skeletal muscle mitophagy
- Need: muscle biopsy studies with mitochondrial respiration
- Gold standard: mt-Keima reporter imaging, electron microscopy

#### 4.2.2 Bioavailability
- Resveratrol: >90% first-pass metabolism
- Spermidine: rapid systemic clearance
- Urolithin A: gut microbiome variability
- Solution: novel delivery systems, prodrug approaches

#### 4.2.3 Target Specificity
- All current activators induce general autophagy
- Mitophagy-specific targets (PINK1, Parkin) not yet druggable
- Therapeutic window concerns

#### 4.2.4 Clinical Trial Design
- Outcome measures: SPPB, grip strength, DXA lean mass
- Population: EWGSOP2-diagnosed sarcopenia
- Duration: ≥6 months for functional outcomes

### 4.3 Future Directions
1. Muscle biopsy RCTs with mitochondrial endpoints
2. Combination approaches (mitophagy + exercise)
3. Precision medicine (biomarker-guided responders)
4. Head-to-head comparisons

### 4.4 Clinical Recommendations
- **Outside research:** Insufficient evidence for mitophagy activators in sarcopenia
- **Clinical practice:** Focus on proven interventions (exercise, protein)
- **Research priority:** Urolithin A Phase III with functional endpoints

---

## 5. Conclusion (150-200 words)

Mitophagy activation represents a scientifically compelling strategy for sarcopenia treatment, targeting the root cause of mitochondrial dysfunction in aging muscle. Urolithin A emerges as the most clinically advanced candidate, with two RCTs demonstrating biomarker-level activity, but lacks evidence for clinically meaningful functional improvement. Spermidine benefits from strong epidemiological support and excellent safety but has not been tested in dedicated muscle trials. Novel compounds remain preclinical.

The field awaits a pivotal trial—likely involving Urolithin A with muscle biopsy endpoints and sarcopenia-defined enrollment—to establish whether mitophagy activation can deliver on its mechanistic promise. Until such evidence accumulates, mitophagy-targeted interventions should be considered investigational for sarcopenia treatment.
